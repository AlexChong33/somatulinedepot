US 11,850,410 B2: Automatically Retracting Safety Needle Assembly

1. Executive Summary

This patent (US 11,850,410 B2) details an automatically retracting safety needle assembly with distal and proximal ends that solves a critical usability problem in prior art: the abrupt increase in resistive force required to activate the safety mechanism. The assembly comprises:

  • A housing with internal housing (IH) and base wall
  • Moving sleeve (MOS) with flange wall, axially movable within IH
  • Biasing element (BE) within IH biasing MOS proximally
  • Syringe receiving plunger rod, movable within IH and coupled to MOS
  • Activation fork (AF) partially within IH with guiding arm coupled to IH and MOS

The core innovation is a mechanism that ensures a substantially constant plunger force throughout the injection and activation sequence (FIGS. 28-29 comparison). This compels the user to complete the full dose before the needle automatically retracts via the retraction mechanism, enhancing patient safety and ensuring dose accuracy. The invention achieves this through precise interaction between:

  • Plunger-actuated Activation Fork (AF) that moves distally with plunger
  • Spring-loaded Moving Sleeve (MOS) that holds the syringe
  • Internal housing geometry that guides component interactions

Once the plunger reaches the end of its travel, the AF moves into a position that "un-gates" the MOS (via alignment of openings/features), allowing the biasing element to retract the entire syringe and needle safely into the housing.

2. Core Problem Solved & Novelty

Primary Problem Solved: Conventional auto-retracting syringes often require the user to apply a significant final push (overcoming an "abrupt increase in resistive force") to trigger the retraction mechanism. An inexperienced user may mistake this resistance for the end of the injection, prematurely stop pushing, and fail to deliver the full dose and/or activate the safety feature.

Key Novelty & Highlight: The invention's primary inventive step is the elimination of this final resistance spike. As shown in the patent's comparison of FIG. 28 (prior art) vs. FIG. 29 (the invention), the force required by the user remains relatively flat until the very end of the plunger's travel. The retraction is triggered not by overcoming a force barrier, but by the plunger and Activation Fork reaching a specific *geometric position* that releases a pre-loaded spring. This makes the device more intuitive, reliable, and safe.

3. Structural Breakdown & Key Components

The assembly is a system of nested and interacting cylindrical components. The table below breaks down the main parts and their functions based on the First Embodiment (FIGS. 1-14) and Second Embodiment (FIGS. 15-27).

Component Reference #(s) Function
External Housing (EH) 101, 201 The outermost shell that the user holds. Provides structural integrity, finger flanges (113, 213), and viewing windows (111, 211).
Internal Housing (IH) 102, 202 Fixed inside the EH. It acts as the primary guide and structural frame for the moving internal parts. It contains features like guiding ribs, protrusions, and a base wall for the spring to push against.
Moving Sleeve (MOS) 103 (ROS), 203 (LOS) The core moving part that holds the syringe. It is biased proximally (towards the user) by the spring. It is held in a "ready" state by the Activation Fork. The patent shows two main versions: a Rotating Sleeve (ROS) and a Locking Sleeve (LOS).
Biasing Element (BE) 104, 204 A compression spring that provides the energy for retraction. It is loaded between the stationary IH base wall and the flange wall of the MOS.
Activation Fork (AF) 105, 205 This is the "trigger" or "gatekeeper". It is coupled to the syringe plunger and moves distally (away from the user) with it. Its guiding arms (151, 251) physically block the MOS from retracting. At the end of the stroke, openings (252, 255) on the AF align with features on the MOS, allowing the MOS to move and initiate retraction.
Syringe 106, 206 A standard-type syringe with a barrel, plunger rod (161, 261), and needle (165, 265). It is coupled to and moves with the MOS.

4. Mechanism of Action (Step-by-Step)

The operation can be understood in four key phases (based on the LOS embodiment, FIGS. 20-27):

  1. Storage/Ready State: The spring (BE 204) is compressed, pushing on the Locking Sleeve (LOS 203). However, the LOS is prevented from moving proximally because its gripping snaps (232) are blocked from deflecting outward by the solid body of the Activation Fork's arms (251). The system is locked and ready for use.
  2. Injection Phase: The user pushes the plunger rod (261). This simultaneously expels the medication and advances the attached Activation Fork (AF 205) distally. During this entire phase, the AF continues to block the LOS, and the resistive force felt by the user is simply the force required to push the fluid through the needle.
  3. Activation (Geometric Trigger): As the plunger reaches the end of its travel, the Activation Fork (AF) has moved distally to a point where its pre-cut activation openings (252) are now perfectly aligned with the LOS gripping snaps (232). The AF is no longer blocking them.
  4. Retraction & Locking Phase: The instant the LOS snaps are un-gated, the stored energy in the spring (BE 204) takes over. The spring pushes the LOS (and the attached syringe) proximally. The angled slopes (225) on the stationary IH cause the LOS snaps (232) to deflect outward through the now-aligned openings (252) in the AF, allowing the syringe to retract fully into the housing. Once retracted, other features on the LOS and IH engage to permanently lock the needle inside, preventing re-exposure and further movement.
